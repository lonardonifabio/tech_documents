This research investigates the rapidly developing field of artificial intelligence (AI) ethics and governance auditing. The study, based on an interview-based survey of 34 individuals across seven countries, explores the motivations, core activities, and obstacles encountered by those involved in AI ethics auditing within the private sector. The findings reveal that AI ethics audits often mirror financial auditing processes but are frequently hampered by a lack of comprehensive stakeholder engagement, inadequate measurement of success metrics, and a deficiency in external reporting. A key observation is the audit's concentrated focus on technical aspects of AI ethics, specifically bias, privacy, and explainability, driven by a regulatory emphasis on technical risk management. Auditors grapple with numerous challenges, including juggling demands from diverse functional areas, resource limitations (staffing and infrastructure), and the ambiguity surrounding regulatory guidance due to the absence of established best practices. The research highlights the crucial, albeit nascent, role that AI ethics and governance auditors are currently playing in proactively addressing ethical concerns within the AI landscape. The study underscores the need for improved frameworks, standardized methodologies, and clearer regulatory standards to facilitate more effective and impactful AI ethics audits.
