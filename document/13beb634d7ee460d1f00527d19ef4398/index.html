Deepfakes and Cheap Fakes: The Manipulation of Audio and Visual Evidence

Authors: Britt Paris, Joan Donovan

This report examines the growing threat of 'deepfakes' and 'cheap fakes' – manipulated audio and visual evidence – and their impact on information and trust. It explores the evolution of these techniques, starting with early examples of AI-manipulated face swap videos appearing in November 2017. The document highlights how these technologies, increasingly accessible and affordable, are being used to create convincing forgeries, posing a significant challenge to the veracity of evidence. It delves into the 'cheap fakes' spectrum, encompassing various methods like photoshopping and lookalikes, alongside more sophisticated deepfake techniques involving face swapping and voice synthesis. The report argues that while the technology is new, the underlying struggle for power and manipulation is not, simply manifesting in a new digital landscape. The document raises critical questions about whether deepfakes represent an 'information apocalypse' and the potential end of evidence as we understand it. It emphasizes the need to understand the nuances of contemporary AV manipulation and the historical context of power dynamics surrounding information control. The research is conducted under Data & Society’s Media Manipulation research initiative, focusing on the implications of these technologies for society and the future of truth. The report’s findings contribute to a broader understanding of the challenges posed by manipulated media and the importance of critical thinking and verification in the digital age. The authors, Britt Paris and Joan Donovan, bring expertise from Rutgers University and Harvard Kennedy School respectively, adding depth to the analysis.
