Perception and Evaluation of AI-Generated Medical Responses by Nonexperts

Authors: Ms. Shekar, Dr. Pataranutaporn

This article investigates the public's perception and evaluation of medical responses created by artificial intelligence (AI) versus those produced by human medical doctors. The study involved 300 participants who assessed medical responses, with some responses originating from an online healthcare platform and others generated by a large language model and validated by physicians. The research revealed a significant trend: participants struggled to differentiate between AI-generated and doctor-written responses, exhibiting a clear preference for AI-generated responses, particularly those labeled as ‘high accuracy.’ These high-accuracy AI responses were perceived as more valid, trustworthy, and satisfactory. Notably, even low-accuracy AI-generated responses elicited a similar level of acceptance, with participants finding them acceptable. However, a concerning outcome emerged: participants demonstrated a tendency to blindly follow the advice provided by these AI responses, leading to an increased likelihood of seeking unnecessary medical attention. This reaction highlighted a critical vulnerability – the potential for AI-generated medical advice to be accepted without critical evaluation, raising serious concerns about patient safety and the need for safeguards to prevent misuse. The study underscores the importance of understanding how non-experts interpret and react to AI-driven medical information, particularly regarding accuracy and potential risks. Further research is needed to explore the underlying cognitive biases that contribute to this acceptance and to develop strategies for mitigating the potential harms associated with AI-generated medical advice. The findings have significant implications for the design and deployment of AI in healthcare, emphasizing the necessity for transparency, validation, and user education.
