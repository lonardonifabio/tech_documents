The Automated but Risky Game: Modeling Agent-to-Agent Negotiations and Transactions in Consumer Markets

Authors: Shenzhe Zhu, Jiao Sun, Yi Nian, Tobin South, Alex Pentland, Jiaxin Pei

This research paper explores the potential and risks of utilizing AI agents to automate negotiations and transactions within consumer markets. The study investigates a future scenario where both consumers and merchants employ AI agents to manage these interactions. The core focus is on understanding the performance variations among different Large Language Model (LLM) agents when acting as negotiators and the potential for financial risks associated with full automation. The authors designed an experimental framework to assess the capabilities of various LLM agents in realistic negotiation and transaction scenarios. Their analysis revealed a significant imbalance in deal-making performance, with different AI agents exhibiting substantial disparities in securing favorable outcomes for their respective users. A key finding highlighted the possibility of behavioral anomalies within LLMs, which could lead to financial losses for both consumers and merchants when these agents are integrated into real-world decision-making processes. The research underscores the need for careful consideration and mitigation strategies when deploying AI agents in consumer markets, particularly concerning the potential for unexpected or detrimental outcomes. The study contributes to a growing body of work examining the ethical and practical implications of AI-driven automation in commerce. The authors' experimental design and findings provide valuable insights for developers, policymakers, and businesses seeking to understand and manage the complexities of AI-mediated transactions. Further research is needed to develop robust safeguards and strategies to ensure responsible and beneficial use of AI agents in consumer negotiations and transactions. The paper's emphasis on behavioral anomalies is particularly pertinent, as it highlights the importance of monitoring and controlling the actions of AI agents to prevent unintended financial consequences.
