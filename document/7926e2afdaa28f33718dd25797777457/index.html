Mathematics of Machine Learning

Authors: Martin Lotz

This document, "Mathematics of Machine Learning," authored by Martin Lotz, provides a comprehensive exploration of the mathematical foundations underpinning machine learning algorithms. Published in March 2020 by the Mathematics Institute at The University of Warwick, the book systematically covers key concepts and techniques. The core of the book is divided into three main sections: "Statistical Learning Theory," "Optimization," and "Deep Learning." The first section delves into statistical learning theory, focusing on binary classification, finite hypothesis sets, probably approximately correct methods, and fundamental concepts like Rademacher complexity and VC theory. It examines the VC inequality and covering numbers, alongside model selection strategies. The second section, "Optimization," concentrates on the mathematical tools used to train machine learning models. This includes a detailed treatment of optimization techniques, convexity, Lagrangian duality, and KKT conditions. Crucially, it explores the application of these methods to Support Vector Machines (SVMs), covering both their initial implementation and iterative algorithms. The book also addresses convergence analysis and extensions of gradient descent, including Stochastic Gradient Descent. Finally, the third section, "Deep Learning," investigates neural networks, universal approximation theorems, and Convolutional Neural Networks. It further explores robustness and the emerging field of Generative Adversarial Networks. The document emphasizes the mathematical rigor required to understand and develop machine learning systems, highlighting the importance of concepts like gradient descent and its various extensions. The book’s structure and content are designed to provide a solid mathematical basis for anyone involved in machine learning research or development. The introductory quote from Marvin Minsky underscores the inherent complexity and often opaque nature of computer systems, framing learning as a process of transforming data into knowledge and understanding, ultimately enabling independent task performance.
