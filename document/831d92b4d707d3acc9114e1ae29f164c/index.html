Large language Models (LLMs) - A Backgrounder

Authors: Unknown

This document provides a backgrounder on Large Language Models (LLMs), a specific type of Foundation Model within the broader field of Generative AI. LLMs are deep learning models trained on massive datasets of text, including books, articles, websites, and code. These models utilize complex statistical analysis to identify patterns and connections within the data, enabling them to generate entirely new text. The core function of LLMs is to analyze vast datasets, identify patterns and connections in the data between words and phrases, and leverage these to eventually generate completely new text. Their applications are diverse, ranging from powering conversational AI systems that deliver contextually relevant responses, to automatically summarizing complex documents into concise synopses, facilitating seamless language translations, and even generating intricate code snippets. The development of LLMs has a rich history, beginning with early neural network experiments in the 1950s, notably with Joseph Weizenbaum's creation of Eliza, a primitive chatbot. Eliza’s development highlighted the potential of natural language processing (NLP) and served as a foundational step towards the sophisticated LLMs currently in use. Later advancements, such as the introduction of Long Short-Term (LSTM) networks, further propelled the evolution of these models. The document essentially serves as an introductory overview of LLMs, outlining their origins, underlying technology, and current applications, emphasizing their role in transforming various industries and technological domains. It establishes LLMs as a key component of Generative AI and highlights the continuous innovation driving their development.
