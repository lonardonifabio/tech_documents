Artificial Intelligence, Human Capital, and Innovation

Authors: Michael Gofman, Zhao Jin

This research investigates the critical role of human capital, specifically the movement of AI faculty, in driving innovation, particularly within the context of artificial intelligence. The study highlights a significant 'brain drain' of AI professors from North American universities into the industry between 2004 and 2018, driven by the scarcity of specialized human capital needed for AI research and development. The authors present causal evidence demonstrating that this departure of AI faculty directly reduced the creation of startups by students who had graduated from these universities. Furthermore, these departures negatively impacted the early-stage funding received by these graduates' startups. The core mechanism identified is the disruption of knowledge transfer – the crucial link between experienced professors and aspiring students – as the primary channel through which human capital reallocation hinders innovation. The research underscores the importance of maintaining a robust flow of knowledge and expertise within academic institutions to foster innovation in AI. The study relies on data analysis to establish this causal relationship, emphasizing the delicate balance between utilizing human capital for innovation and its application in education. The authors acknowledge financial support from the Ewing Marion Kauffman Foundation (Grant Number: G-201806-4434) and access to Crunchbase's database, indicating a comprehensive approach to understanding the dynamics of AI innovation and its dependence on human capital. The research contributes to the broader discussion on the strategic allocation of human resources within the rapidly evolving field of artificial intelligence. The JEL classification codes (L26, O33, O31, I23, J24) further categorize the research within economics and innovation studies.
