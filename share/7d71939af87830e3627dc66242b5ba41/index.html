Issue Brief: January 2025 - AI Incidents: Key Components for a Mandatory Reporting Regime

Authors: Ren Bin Lee, Dixion Heather Frase

This issue brief, released in January 2025, outlines the essential components needed for a mandatory AI incident reporting regime. Building upon a previous publication advocating for a federated AI incident reporting framework, it details a standardized set of key components to systematically record, analyze, and respond to artificial intelligence incidents. The document proposes a hybrid approach incorporating mandatory, voluntary, and citizen reporting mechanisms. Specifically, it defines a template for collecting vital AI incident data, encompassing details such as the type of incident, the nature and severity of harm, relevant technical data, affected entities and individuals, and the contextual circumstances surrounding the event. The framework draws upon existing AI initiatives, real-world events, and lessons learned from incident reporting systems across sectors including transportation, healthcare, and cybersecurity. The goal is to establish a robust and comprehensive approach to managing and mitigating risks associated with AI systems. The brief intentionally maintains a high-level perspective while providing a foundational structure for detailed incident reporting. It emphasizes the need for a standardized approach to ensure consistent data collection and effective response strategies. The document serves as a guide for developing and implementing a mandatory AI incident reporting system, promoting accountability and facilitating proactive risk management in the rapidly evolving field of artificial intelligence.

Key Concepts:

  • Implementation of a federated and comprehensive AI incident reporting framework
  • Standardized key components of AI incidents (type, harm, technical data, affected entities, context)
  • Integration of lessons learned from transportation, healthcare, and cybersecurity sectors
  • Identifying commonalities and gaps in AI incident components
  • Pragmatic and adaptable taxonomies for incident data collection
  • Differentiating between AI near misses and hazards (as defined by the OECD)
  • AI systems and their deployment
  • Assessment of safety and security measures
  • Identification of gaps for improvement
  • Relationship between stakeholders and AI systems
