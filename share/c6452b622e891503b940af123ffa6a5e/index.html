HOW GENERATIVE ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE COULD AFFECT WORK IN THE UK – AND HOW TO MANAGE IT

Authors: Carsten Jung, Bhargav Srinivasa Desikan

This report, published by the Institute for Public Policy Research (IPPR) in March 2024, examines the potential impact of generative artificial intelligence (AI) on the workforce in the United Kingdom. IPPR, a long-standing independent charity dedicated to creating a fairer, greener, and more prosperous society, has a history of impactful research and policy recommendations spanning over 30 years, addressing issues such as the minimum wage, regional inequality, and energy taxation. The core focus of this paper is to analyze how the rise of generative AI – specifically, AI capable of creating new content like text, images, and code – could reshape employment landscapes. It delves into the potential for both job displacement and the creation of new roles, acknowledging the significant disruption this technology represents. The report doesn't just present a theoretical assessment; it also proposes strategies for managing this transition effectively. This includes considering the need for retraining programs, exploring new social safety nets, and potentially rethinking the relationship between work and income. IPPR’s approach is rooted in practical solutions, aiming to translate bold ideas into tangible policy recommendations. The document highlights the urgency of addressing these challenges proactively, given the rapid advancements in generative AI. The authors emphasize the importance of a nuanced understanding of the technology's capabilities and limitations, alongside a commitment to ensuring that the benefits of AI are shared broadly across society, rather than concentrated in the hands of a few. The report’s findings are intended to inform policymakers and stakeholders as they grapple with the complex implications of generative AI for the UK economy and its citizens. It’s a critical analysis within the context of IPPR’s broader mission to drive progressive change and shape policy debates.
