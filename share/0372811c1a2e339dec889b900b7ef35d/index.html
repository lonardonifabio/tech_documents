Computer Vision Fundamentals for Business Leaders

Authors: Sathesh Sriskandarajah, Peter Malan, Matt Kuperholz

This document, published by PwC in April 2020, serves as an introductory guide for business leaders seeking to understand and leverage Computer Vision. It highlights how Computer Vision, driven by machine learning, is transforming our perception of the world and providing unprecedented intelligence across various business domains, including people, products, assets, and documents. The core focus is on equipping business leaders with the knowledge to navigate the opportunities and risks associated with this technology. The document outlines fundamental concepts of Computer Vision, detailing its applications and the challenges involved in implementation. It specifically addresses the responsible use of AI, emphasizing the need for careful risk management alongside the potential benefits. The structure includes an introduction to Artificial Intelligence, a deep dive into Computer Vision fundamentals, exploration of its diverse applications, guidance on getting started, and a critical assessment of the challenges and risks. Furthermore, it incorporates a section on Responsible AI, reflecting the growing importance of ethical considerations in the deployment of AI technologies. The document is authored by PwC experts, including Sathesh Sriskandarajah (Senior Manager, Risk Assurance), Peter Malan (Partner, Digital Trust), and Matt Kuperholz (Partner, Chief Data Scientist), demonstrating PwC's commitment to providing practical insights for business leaders in the age of intelligent automation. The publication emphasizes the transformative potential of computer vision and the need for a strategic approach to its adoption.

Key Concepts:

  • Computer Vision powered by machine learning will change the way we see the world.
  • Organizations can collect unprecedented intelligence about their people, products, assets, and documents.
  • Understanding and managing the risks associated with implementing Computer Vision.
  • Extensive customer tracking through camera surveillance.
  • Integration of in-store tracking with customer data.
  • The future of retail driven by automation and digital technologies.
  • The need for infrastructure to support computer vision models.
  • The importance of high-quality labelled training data for complex phenomena.
  • Evaluating the potential business value of a computer vision solution.
