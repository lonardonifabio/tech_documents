50 years of Data Science

Authors: David Donoho

This document, authored by David Donoho, reflects on the evolution of 'Data Science' over the past 50 years. It traces the roots of the field back to John Tukey's call for a reformation of academic statistics in 1955, articulated in his influential paper, 'The Future of Data Analysis.' Tukey recognized the potential for a new science focused on learning from data ‚Äì data analysis. Decades later, figures like John Chambers, Bill Cleveland, and Leo Breiman independently championed expanding the scope of statistics beyond traditional theoretical models, with Chambers advocating for greater emphasis on data preparation and presentation, and Breiman prioritizing prediction over inference. Cleveland is credited with coining the term "Data Science." The document highlights the recent surge in 'Data Science' programs at leading universities ‚Äì including UC Berkeley, NYU, MIT, and the University of Michigan ‚Äì exemplified by the University of Michigan's recently announced $100M 'Data Science Initiative,' which includes hiring 35 new faculty. These new programs exhibit significant overlap with traditional statistics curricula, yet they largely operate independently from established academic statistics departments. The excerpt provides a snapshot of the current 'Data Science moment,' referencing contemporary discussions and the growing prominence of data science as a distinct field. It sets the stage for a deeper exploration of the key developments and trends shaping the field.
