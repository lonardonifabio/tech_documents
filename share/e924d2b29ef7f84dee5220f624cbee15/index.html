Data Science Interview Questions (30 days of Interview Preparation)

Authors: INEURON.AI

This document excerpt provides a foundational overview of key concepts frequently tested in data science interviews, specifically focusing on the distinctions between Artificial Intelligence (AI), Data Science, Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL). It begins by defining AI as a computational field that aims to solve human problems, evolving from purely mathematical exercises to mimicking human capabilities. AI is further categorized into General AI (focused on planning, decision-making, and mimicking human tasks) and Applied AI (such as autonomous vehicles and algorithmic trading). Machine Learning (ML) is presented as a form of AI where computers learn from data without explicit programming, enabling adaptive and scalable solutions, exemplified by navigation apps and recommendation systems. ML is then broken down into three main types: Supervised Learning, Unsupervised Learning, and Reinforcement Learning. Finally, the document introduces Data Science, highlighting its multidisciplinary nature, utilizing tools and techniques from AI, ML, and other fields to handle large datasets. The core objective of data science aligns with machine learning, aiming to extract actionable insights and build predictive models from data. The document serves as a concise introduction to these concepts, commonly assessed during data science interview preparation. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of these fields and their application in solving real-world problems.

Key Concepts:

  • AI is purely math and scientific exercise, evolving into computational problem-solving.
  • Machine Learning allows computers to learn without explicit programming, adapting based on data.
  • Data Science utilizes tools and techniques from AI, ML, and other fields to handle large datasets and achieve accurate insights.
  • R-squared measures the proportion of variance explained by a linear model.
  • A high R-squared value indicates a good model fit, but can be inflated by adding irrelevant variables.
  • The Adjusted R-square provides a better measure of model fit by penalizing the inclusion of unnecessary variables.
  • Mean Squared Error quantifies the average squared difference between observed and predicted values.
