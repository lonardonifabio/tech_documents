HUMAN PREFERENCES IN LARGE LANGUAGE MODEL LATENT SPACE

Authors: Sarah Ball, Simeon Allmendinger, Frauke Kreuter, Niklas Kühl

This research paper presents a technical analysis investigating the reliability of synthetic data generated by large language models (LLMs) in the context of survey-based predictions, specifically focusing on human preferences. The study addresses a critical gap in the field by examining how demographic attributes and prompt variations impact the latent opinion mappings within LLMs. Utilizing 14 different LLMs, the authors conduct a rigorous evaluation of synthetic data's ability to replicate the variance present in genuine human responses. The core finding is that LLM-generated data consistently fails to accurately reproduce the observed diversity of opinions, particularly when considering demographic subgroups. Specifically, the research demonstrates a significant limitation in the political domain, where persona-to-party mappings exhibit a lack of differentiation, resulting in synthetic data that lacks the complex and nuanced distribution of opinions typically found in real-world survey data. Furthermore, the study highlights the substantial influence of prompt sensitivity on LLM outputs, indicating that variations in prompts can dramatically alter the generated responses, thereby compromising the stability and predictive capabilities of the synthetic data. This underscores the challenges associated with relying on LLMs as substitutes for human respondents in survey applications. The research contributes valuable insights into the technical limitations of GenAI in survey contexts and emphasizes the need for further investigation into methods for improving the fidelity and reliability of synthetic data generated by LLMs. The findings have important implications for researchers and practitioners utilizing GenAI in survey design and analysis, urging a cautious approach to interpreting and applying synthetic data.
