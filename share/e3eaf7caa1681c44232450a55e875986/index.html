Towards a Common Reporting Framework for AI Incidents

Authors: Karine Perset, Luis Aranda, Bénédicte Rispal, Audrey Plonk

This OECD Artificial Intelligence Paper presents a proposed common reporting framework for AI incidents, designed to establish a global benchmark for stakeholders across various jurisdictions and sectors. The core objective is to facilitate a standardized approach to reporting AI incidents, enabling countries to adopt a consistent methodology while retaining the flexibility to tailor responses to their specific domestic policies and regulations. The framework’s primary value lies in providing policymakers with enhanced insights into the nature and scope of AI incidents occurring in diverse contexts. Specifically, it seeks to identify high-risk AI systems, meticulously assess their potential impacts, and proactively understand emerging risks associated with AI deployment. The development of this report involved extensive consultation and review processes, including discussions and revisions by the OECD.AI Expert Group on AI Incidents during its meetings in February, April, June, and October 2024. Furthermore, the OECD Working Party on Artificial Intelligence (AIGO) examined the report at its June 2024 meeting, and the Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) considered the work during its November 2024 Plenary. The report’s creation benefited from valuable input from delegates within the Global Partnership on AI (GPAI) and the OECD Working Party on Artificial Intelligence (AIGO), including the Civil Society Information Society Advisory Council. The collaborative nature of this project underscores the importance of international cooperation in addressing the challenges and risks posed by artificial intelligence. The document represents a significant step towards a more coordinated and informed approach to managing AI incidents globally.

Key Concepts:

  • A common reporting approach for AI incidents across jurisdictions.
  • Establishing a global benchmark for stakeholders in AI reporting.
  • Identifying high-risk AI systems and assessing their impacts.
  • Common reporting framework for AI incidents
  • Recurrent criteria (criteria appearing in at least three frameworks)
  • Complementary criteria (criteria not covered by recurrent criteria)
  • Consistency in reporting and comparability
  • Predefined responses for reporting criteria
  • Alignment with the OECD Framework for the Classification of AI systems
  • Alternative reporting interfaces and mandatory criteria adjustments
