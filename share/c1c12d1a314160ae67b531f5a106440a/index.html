Engineering at Anthropic: How we built our multi-agent research system

Authors: Anthropic

This document details the engineering process behind Anthropic's multi-agent research system, leveraging multiple Claude agents to tackle complex research topics. The system, introduced as part of Anthropic's Research feature, utilizes a core agent that plans a research process based on user queries, subsequently deploying parallel agents to concurrently search across the web, Google Workspace, and integrated tools. The development journey, progressing from prototype to production, highlighted critical lessons concerning system architecture, tool design, and prompt engineering. A key characteristic of this multi-agent system is its dynamic nature, relying on autonomous LLMs working in a loop with tools, adapting research approaches based on emergent leads and continuous discovery. The document emphasizes the challenges associated with coordinating multiple agents, evaluating their performance, and ensuring system reliability. It outlines the principles that guided Anthropic's development, aiming to provide valuable insights for others building their own multi-agent systems. The system's design is particularly suited for open-ended research problems where predefined paths are ineffective due to the inherent dynamism and path-dependency of the investigation process. The post serves as a practical guide, sharing the lessons learned and offering a framework for building robust and adaptable multi-agent research systems. The core concept revolves around empowering agents to autonomously explore and refine their approach based on real-time findings, mirroring the iterative nature of human research.

Key Concepts:

  • Multi-agent systems consist of multiple agents (LLMs autonomously using tools in a loop) working together.
  • The research feature utilizes Claude agents to explore complex topics more effectively by searching across the web and Google Workspace.
  • Dynamic and path-dependent research processes are inherent in open-ended problems, requiring adaptable approaches.
  • Flexible evaluation methods for multi-agent systems that focus on achieving desired outcomes rather than prescribed steps.
  • Importance of immediate evaluation with small samples during early agent development.
  • The challenge of evaluating multi-agent systems due to agents taking diverse paths to achieve goals.
