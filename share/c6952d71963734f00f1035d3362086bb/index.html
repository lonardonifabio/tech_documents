Children and Deepfakes

Authors: Mar Negreiro

This EPRS briefing from the European Parliamentary Research Service examines the rapidly growing threat of deepfakes, particularly their impact on children. Deepfakes, generated using artificial intelligence (AI), are becoming increasingly prevalent and accessible, moving beyond specialized tools to free mobile applications. The document highlights the alarming rise in deepfake creation and sharing, projecting 8 million deepfakes to be shared in 2025, a significant increase from 500,000 in 2023. A key concern is the disproportionate risk posed to children due to their developing cognitive abilities and increased vulnerability to online harms. The briefing emphasizes that children are less equipped to discern deepfakes and are more susceptible to grooming, cyberbullying, and child sexual abuse material. The European Commission reports that a substantial majority (approximately 98%) of existing deepfakes are utilized for the creation of pornographic content. The document stresses the urgent need for legal action and international cooperation to combat this evolving threat. It underscores the importance of developing effective detection tools and methods, alongside proactive measures to safeguard children from the potential harms associated with deepfakes. The rapid advancement of AI technology is outpacing existing legal frameworks, creating a significant challenge for regulators and policymakers. Furthermore, the briefing calls for enhanced awareness campaigns and educational initiatives to equip children with the critical thinking skills necessary to navigate the increasingly deceptive digital landscape. The document implicitly advocates for a multi-faceted approach, combining technological solutions with legal frameworks and public awareness campaigns, to mitigate the risks posed by deepfakes to vulnerable populations, particularly children.

Key Concepts:

  • Deepfakes are videos, images, and audio created using AI to simulate or fabricate content.
  • The increasing accessibility and sophistication of deepfakes pose significant risks, particularly regarding the spread of misinformation and non-consensual content.
  • Children are particularly vulnerable to deepfakes due to their developing cognitive abilities and susceptibility to online harms.
  • Impersonation of family members for fraudulent transactions
  • Use of deepfakes for misleading video calls and information manipulation
  • Lack of understanding of privacy among children regarding AI interactions
  • Labeling obligation for AI-generated content
  • Human review and editorial control mitigating labeling requirements
  • Risk mitigation measures for large online platforms
  • Protection of victims from deepfakes
