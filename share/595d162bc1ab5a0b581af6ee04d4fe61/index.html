Generative AI and Copyright Training, Creation, Regulation

Authors: Nicola LUCCHI

This study, commissioned by the European Parliament’s Policy Department for Justice, Civil Liberties and Institutional Affairs at the request of the Committee on Legal Affairs, delves into the significant challenges posed by generative AI to the foundations of EU copyright law. The core issue identified is the existing legal framework’s incompatibility with the practices used in training generative AI models. Specifically, the report highlights a critical mismatch between current text and data mining exceptions and the methods employed by AI developers. This discrepancy creates uncertainty surrounding the legal status of AI-generated content, raising substantial structural risks for the future of creativity within Europe. The report emphasizes the importance of a robust legal framework to safeguard Europe’s rich and diverse cultural heritage, which relies on the continued protection and fair remuneration of authors. The document advocates for the implementation of several key reforms, including establishing clear distinctions between input and output data, the adoption of harmonized opt-out mechanisms for data usage, and the imposition of transparency obligations on AI training processes. Furthermore, it proposes the development of equitable licensing models to ensure that authors receive appropriate compensation for the use of their works in AI training. The European Parliament is expected to take a leading role in driving these changes, recognizing the need to balance innovation in the field of generative AI with the fundamental rights of authors. The study underscores the urgency of addressing these issues to foster a sustainable and equitable ecosystem for creativity and technological advancement. The document’s findings are crucial for informing policy discussions and shaping legal frameworks that can effectively navigate the complexities of AI-generated content and its impact on the creative industries. The report’s recommendations aim to provide a pathway for a balanced approach, ensuring that innovation thrives while respecting intellectual property rights and supporting the continued vitality of European culture.

Key Concepts:

  • Legal mismatch between AI training practices and current text and data mining exceptions
  • Uncertain status of AI-generated content
  • Harmonised opt-out mechanisms
  • Equitable licensing models
  • Evolving realities of creativity, authorship, and machine-generated expression
  • Value Gap: The absence of mechanisms for compensating creators whose works are used to train AI models.
  • Incentive Structure: Copyright relies on an incentive structure that is undermined by the current lack of compensation for AI training.
  • Statutory Remuneration Scheme: A proposed solution involving a collective licence or levy to ensure creators are compensated.
  • Large language models process text statistically to generate coherent outputs without true understanding.
  • The legal implications of robots as innovators and creators.
